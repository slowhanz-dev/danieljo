Why it matters who makes it



The brief ends.
The object remains.

Most designers deliver a file. What arrives at the factory is often something else entirely. Daniel Jo is one of the few designers who understands both ends of that journey — and controls them.

01

The material decides

Porcelain is not a passive medium. It shrinks, warps, and rejects shortcuts. Every design decision here begins with what the material will and won't allow — not what looks good on screen.

02

Digital where it must be exact

Tolerances, stacking geometry, wall thickness — these are problems Rhino 3D solves better than the human eye. We use digital tools not as an aesthetic, but as a necessity. Precision is not a style choice.

03

The hand where the machine loses

A lathe-turned cylinder achieves the same precision as CNC at a fraction of the cost. Knowing where handcraft wins — and using it there — is not romanticism. It is engineering.

04

Proportion at 0.5mm

A single handle is prototyped twenty to thirty times. Form dimensions are tested at half-millimetre intervals. The cup you reach for without thinking — that instinct was designed, not discovered.

05

One studio, full cycle

Brief to 3D model. Prototype to ODM. Tooling to final glaze. Nothing leaves the process without being understood first. Over 500 café clients. Buyers in three continents. The pipeline works.

06

Objects that earn their place

A well-made object doesn't ask to be noticed. It simply outlasts everything around it. The goal is never a beautiful render — it is something that still makes sense in ten years, on a table that hasn't been designed yet.

Twenty years.
One material.
No shortcuts.

Daniel Jo has been making ceramic products since 2004 — not as an artist exploring a medium, but as a designer who chose the hardest one and learned to control it.

The client list reads like a brief history of when Korean design began to matter internationally: MoMA Store New York, Naver, Amore Pacific, Osulloc, 10 Corso Como Seoul, Kyouei Design Japan. Each project built on the same foundation — a material understood deeply enough to be pushed precisely.

Since 2020, Bunnar has placed ceramic tableware in over 500 cafés across Korea. The same cups are now ordered by buyers in the Netherlands, Australia, and Japan. Not because of marketing. Because the proportions are right.
